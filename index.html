Shield Laws Timeline

How the absence of federal protections reshapes investigative journalism in the United States.

What Is A Shield Law?

Shield laws are legislative laws intended to protect journalists from having to reveal their sources or unpublished material. Such laws allow journalists to do their job without the fear of courtroom trials, and potentially serving jail time.

Why Federal Jurisdiction Matters

As of today, most U.S. states have protective shield laws in place. However, these shield laws do not extend to federal courts. Federal court cases involve large crime, national security cases, and higher level corruption. therefore, journalists face greater legal risk when reporting on high impact stories.

State vs. Federal Divide

While 49 states and the district of Columbia have enacted some form of shield law, these protections vary in scope and effectiveness. Some states offer absolute protection for journalists, while others have more limited provisions. This patchwork of laws creates confusion and uncertainty for journalists operating across state lines. On the other hand, federal protections are non-existent. A reporter can still be subpoenaed in federal court.

Alabama
In 1935, the state of Alabama enacted its first shield laws, which provided total privilege to journalists who worked for news stations and newspapers. This was the first instance of a shield law in the United States, and it set a precedent for other states to follow.

Branzburg v. Hayes (1972)
The issue of media protection began to catch attention with the Branzburg v. Hayes in 1972. Newspaper reporters Paul Pappas, Earl Caldwell, and Paul Branzburg were subpoenaed to testify before grand juries to reveal information about what they observed in the Black Panther Party that they haven't disclosed. The three promised their sources confidentiality, and refused to reveal them. The Supreme Court voted 5-4 against the journalists. Stating the First Amendment did not grant reporters special privilege. This case urged Congress to consider protective shield laws for journalists. We quickly begin to see the emergence of bills in Congress.

Congress continues to propose bills for national shield laws for reporters.
In the years following the Branzburg v. Hayes court decision, several bills were introduced in Congress to establish a federal shield law for journalists. However, none of these bills were ever passed into law, and the issue of media protection remains unresolved at the federal level. Overtime, the debate moves the focus to the definition of a 'real reporter', and who should get federal protection.

Privacy Protection Act of 1980
This act was passed to protect journalist from search and seizure of their notes and other materials. However, journalists can still be subpoenaed for search.

The Free Flow of Information Act of 2013
In the 2000s, the main issue regarding a reporter's privilege was the Free Flow of Information Act of 2013. This Act would prohibit the government from pressuring reporters into disclosing their sources unless a Judge decides that after giving the reporter an opportunity to be heard, and if certain criteria are met, to further investigate the reporter. This bill was never passed into law.

Protect Reporters from Exploitative State Spying Act (PRESS Act)
Introduced in 2023, and recieved great support from both parties in the House of Representatives. This Act would create a nation-wide law that stops government officials from forcing sources out of journalists. This Act still remains a bill today.

Impact on Sources

Journalists mainly get thier information from whistleblowers, a term referring to people who expose the wrongdoings of a person or within an organization. When their protection is not guaranteed, they may be discouraged from coming forward, and stop sharing important information with the press. The loss of public knowledge is significant, as it can hinder accountability and transparency in both government and private sectors.

Prosecutorial Leverage

The lack of federal protection for journalists has a few implications. One, grand jury proceedings are confidential under Rule 6(e) of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure. This means that journalists are kept in the dark about why they must testify, and what evidence is already collected. They often lack the investigative context needed to challenge the subpoena. If the journalist refuses to testify, they will face charges, potentially including jail time. If they do testify, they may be forced to reveal their sources.

National Security

When it comes to national security investigations, these are frequently done at the federal level. Shield laws are necessary to protect source confidentiality, while allowing some national security exceptions. Such as a threat to public safety, or risk of disclosure of classified information. For example, in the Pentagon Papers case, the government argued that the publication of classified documents posed a threat to national security.

Emerging Journalism and Digital Media

In the era of the internet and social media, journalists face more challenges when it comes to protecting their sources and delivering news to the public safely and effectively. Data tracking has become a large concern, a common example is the usage of cookies on websites to track your data and online behavior. Another major concern is the increase of attacks on journalists, with TV journalists being the most vulnerable.
